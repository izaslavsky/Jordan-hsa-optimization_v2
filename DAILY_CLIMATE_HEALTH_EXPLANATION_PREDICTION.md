# Daily Climate-Health Explanation and Prediction Exercise

This document describes the daily INF-footprint climate-health modeling exercise implemented in:

- `GEE_local_HSA_Daily_Climate.ipynb`
- `generate_daily_disease_counts.py`
- `prepare_daily_modeling_dataset.py`
- `run_climate_models_daily.ipynb`

The exercise has two linked goals. First, it tests whether daily climate exposures are associated with daily diarrheal disease counts, and whether those associations are modified by sanitation/infrastructure quality. Second, it tests whether the same daily climate information improves short-horizon prediction of future daily diarrheal counts beyond seasonal and autoregressive baselines.

## Data Extraction

### Daily diarrheal outcomes

Daily outcomes are generated by `generate_daily_disease_counts.py` from `data/INF_patient_visits.csv`.

The script filters patient visits to:

- `general_category == "Diarrheal Diseases"`
- dates from `2022-07-01` through `2024-01-31`

Facility-level visits are allocated to HSAs using `out/INF_footprint_facility_hsa_assignments.csv`. Where a facility is probabilistically allocated across multiple HSAs, each visit is expanded into weighted HSA-day records. The output count is therefore a weighted daily count, not necessarily an integer.

The script then builds a complete HSA-by-day panel and fills true no-visit days with zero. Known system reporting gaps are flagged as missing rather than treated as disease-free days:

- `2022-06-02` through `2022-06-30`
- `2023-05-30`
- `2023-05-31`

The resulting daily disease file is:

`out/INF_footprint_daily_diarrheal.csv`

Current size:

| Item | Value |
|---|---:|
| Rows | 11,020 |
| HSAs | 19 |
| Date range | 2022-07-01 to 2024-01-31 |
| Outcome-missing rows flagged as reporting gaps | 38 |

### Daily climate extraction

Daily climate is extracted in `GEE_local_HSA_Daily_Climate.ipynb` using Google Earth Engine. The notebook exports one CSV per HSA footprint, with one row per HSA per calendar day.

The extraction period is:

- `2022-06-01` to `2024-01-31`

The earlier start date provides the 14-day climate lag buffer needed before the first valid modeled outcome date.

The climate sources are:

| Source | Earth Engine collection | Use |
|---|---|---|
| CHIRPS daily | `UCSB-CHG/CHIRPS/DAILY` | Daily precipitation total |
| ERA5-Land hourly | `ECMWF/ERA5_LAND/HOURLY` | Temperature, dewpoint, wind, soil moisture, heat/threshold measures |

For each HSA polygon and each day, the notebook computes HSA-level spatial means using `reduceRegion` with a 9 km ERA5 scale. The exported variables are:

| Variable | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `P_precip` | CHIRPS daily precipitation |
| `T_mean_C` | Daily mean 2 m temperature, Celsius |
| `T_max_C` | Daily max 2 m temperature, Celsius |
| `T_min_C` | Daily min 2 m temperature, Celsius |
| `Td_C` | Daily mean dewpoint, Celsius |
| `DTR_C` | Diurnal temperature range |
| `wind_speed_ms` | 10 m wind speed |
| `SM1` | ERA5 soil moisture layer 1 |
| `SM2` | ERA5 soil moisture layer 2 |
| `hours_above_30C` | Count of hourly ERA5 observations above 30 C |
| `heat_index_C` | Simplified heat index, `T + 0.4 * (Td - T)` |

Output location:

`out/DRIVE_CLIMATE_BY_HSA_DOWNLOAD_DAILY/`

The local folder currently contains 23 CSV files because older exports remain in the folder. The modeling merge is constrained by the current health/HSA panel and produces a 19-HSA modeling dataset.

## Data Integration

The integration script is `prepare_daily_modeling_dataset.py`.

It performs these steps:

1. Loads `out/INF_footprint_daily_diarrheal.csv`.
2. Loads daily HSA climate CSVs from `out/DRIVE_CLIMATE_BY_HSA_DOWNLOAD_DAILY/`.
3. Normalizes HSA names from `FacilityName` to the underscore-separated `hsa_id` convention.
4. Merges health and climate by `hsa_id,date`.
5. Sorts by `hsa_id,date`.
6. Builds within-HSA climate lags from 1 to 14 days.
7. Drops rows without complete lag history, which removes the first 14 modeled days per HSA.
8. Removes reporting-gap and missing-outcome rows.
9. Adds temporal and calendar features.
10. Merges `infra_quality` from `jordan-hsa-dlnm/out/dlnm/hsa_metadata.csv`.

The final modeling file is:

`out/modeling/INF_footprint_daily_modeling_dataset.csv`

## Day-of-Week and Calendar Handling

Day-of-week effects are important because daily health facility attendance is not uniform in Jordan. The validated pattern in the raw attendance data is:

- Friday is the only strong weekly attendance-drop day.
- Saturday behaves like a normal service day and is not treated as a weekend closure.
- Ramadan and the Eid holidays have separate attendance-suppression mechanisms and are modeled separately from day of week.

The model uses:

- `day_of_week` dummies, with Monday as the reference day.
- `is_ramadan`.
- `is_eid_fitr`.
- `is_eid_adha`.
- `is_friday` is retained in the dataset for diagnostics, but Friday is already captured by the day-of-week dummy.

## Track A: Explanatory DLNM

Track A asks whether climate explains daily diarrheal counts after controlling for HSA, seasonality, day-of-week, and major calendar effects. It deliberately excludes autoregressive outcome terms so that the climate signal is not absorbed by recent case counts.

### Model structure

Outcome:

`diarrheal_count`

Model family:

Quasi-Poisson GLM, implemented as Poisson GLM with Pearson `X2` scale.

Base predictors:

- HSA fixed effects.
- Natural cubic spline of `day_of_study`.
- Day-of-week dummies.
- `is_ramadan`, `is_eid_fitr`, `is_eid_adha`.

Precipitation exposure:

- Cross-basis over daily precipitation lags 0 to 14.
- Exposure basis: natural spline with one interior knot.
- Precipitation knot: 80th percentile of non-zero precipitation, 9.45 mm.
- Lag basis: natural spline over lags 0, 3, 7, and 14 days.

Models compared:

1. Base model.
2. Base + precipitation cross-basis.
3. Base + precipitation cross-basis + precipitation cross-basis x centered `infra_quality`.

The interaction model tests whether the precipitation-diarrhea association differs by infrastructure/sanitation quality.

### Main precipitation result

| Test | F | p-value |
|---|---:|---:|
| Precipitation cross-basis vs base | 2.696 | 0.0676 |
| Precipitation cross-basis x infrastructure interaction | 8.213 | 0.0003 |

The fitted interaction model has dispersion `phi = 2.93`.

Interpretation:

- The average precipitation main effect is suggestive but does not pass the 0.05 threshold.
- The interaction with infrastructure quality is statistically strong.
- This supports the interpretation that precipitation-related diarrheal risk is conditional on infrastructure/sanitation context rather than uniform across all HSAs.

## Track B: Predictive Modeling

Track B asks a different question: do daily climate features improve prediction of future diarrheal counts?

The predictive setup uses OLS on:

`log(diarrheal_count + 0.5)`

This is a fast screening model rather than a final operational forecasting model.

### Prediction horizons

The evaluated horizons are:

- 1 day
- 7 days
- 14 days
- 21 days
- 28 days

For each horizon `h`, the model predicts `Y(t + h)` from information available at time `t`.

### Temporal holdout

The holdout is date-based rather than row-based:

- Training dates: first 80% of unique dates.
- Test dates: final 20% of unique dates.
- Split date: `2023-10-11`.
- Training period contains 451 of 564 unique dates.

This matters because the data are sorted by `hsa_id,date`; a row-number split would not be a true temporal split.

### Predictive feature sets

Four models are compared at every horizon:

| Model | Predictors |
|---|---|
| Seasonal-only | HSA fixed effects, seasonal spline, day-of-week, Ramadan/Eid |
| AR+Seasonal | Seasonal-only + lagged outcome at 1 day and 7 days |
| Seasonal+Climate (no AR) | Seasonal-only + climate summaries, no autoregressive terms |
| AR+Seasonal+Climate | AR+Seasonal + climate summaries |

Climate summaries are cross-lag sums over lags 0 to 14 for:

- `P_precip`
- `T_mean_C`
- `Td_C`
- `SM1`

The main early-warning benchmark is `Seasonal+Climate (no AR)`, because it asks whether climate alone improves predictive signal without simply using recent cases.

### Predictive results

Results are saved at:

`out/modeling/daily_models/trackB_horizon_results.csv`

| 7 days | -37.2304 | -28.6199 | -41.5418 | -30.2478 | -1.6279 |
| 14 days | -8.5089 | -5.2479 | -11.4636 | -7.2294 | -1.9815 |
| 21 days | -29.6463 | -25.3270 | -34.6963 | -29.2618 | -3.9348 |

The horizon plot is saved at:

`out/modeling/daily_models/trackB_horizon_R2.png`

Interpretation:

- All holdout R2 values are negative, meaning these simple OLS predictive models perform worse than predicting the holdout mean.
- AR terms improve performance relative to seasonal-only models, but the models still generalize poorly.
- Adding climate summaries worsens holdout R2 at every tested horizon.
- The least negative climate increment is at 7 days, where Delta R2 is -1.6279.
- Therefore, the current climate features are useful for explanatory modeling but not yet useful as implemented for out-of-sample prediction.

This does not mean climate is irrelevant. It means that the current predictive formulation, especially simple summed climate lags in linear OLS, is not an adequate forecasting model. The explanatory DLNM suggests climate-health relationships are nonlinear, lagged, and infrastructure-modified. Track B collapses that structure into simple lag sums, so it is intentionally conservative but likely under-specified.

## Overall Interpretation

The daily exercise separates explanation from prediction.

The explanatory evidence is relatively strong:

- Precipitation alone has a borderline main DLNM effect.
- The precipitation x infrastructure interaction is significant.
- Temperature, dewpoint, soil moisture, and wind also show infrastructure-modified signals.
- These results support a mechanism where climate exposures affect diarrheal disease risk through pathways that are buffered or amplified by sanitation/infrastructure conditions.

The predictive evidence is weak in the current model:

- Simple OLS models on `log(Y + 0.5)` do not generalize to the final 20% of dates.
- Climate summaries do not improve predictive R2 over AR+seasonal baselines.
- The gap between explanatory and predictive results is plausible: climate may influence disease risk mechanistically while still being difficult to use for short-horizon prediction without better outcome dynamics, nonlinear features, regularization, hierarchical structure, and calibration.

## Practical Conclusions

1. The daily extraction and integration pipeline is working end-to-end.
2. The daily modeling matrix is complete for 19 HSAs and 10,716 HSA-days, with 16 HSAs used in fitted models after low-count exclusions.
3. Day-of-week and religious-calendar attendance effects are large and must be controlled.
4. Friday is the major weekly attendance suppression day; Saturday should not be modeled as a closure day.
5. Track A provides useful evidence of infrastructure-modified climate-health associations.
6. Track B should be treated as a diagnostic baseline, not as a deployable forecasting model.

## Suggested Next Modeling Steps

The next predictive iteration should keep the explanatory findings but use a better forecasting design:

- Use regularized Poisson/negative-binomial models instead of unregularized OLS.
- Include nonlinear climate bases or selected DLNM components rather than simple lag sums.
- Fit hierarchical or mixed-effects models to stabilize HSA-level differences.
- Compare against stronger naive baselines, such as same-HSA rolling means and seasonal same-day-of-week baselines.
- Evaluate calibration as well as R2, because count forecasting quality may be poorly summarized by R2 alone.
- Consider separate models by infrastructure tertile or explicit climate x infrastructure features in the prediction track.
