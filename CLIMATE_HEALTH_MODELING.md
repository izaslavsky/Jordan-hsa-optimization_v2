# Climate-Health Modeling: Methods and Results

## Purpose

This modeling exercise assessed the temporal stability and consistency of the optimized HSA networks by fitting predictive models to HSA-aggregated disease data. The analysis examined whether climate variables contribute to explaining weekly disease incidence beyond autoregressive and seasonal effects.

---

## How to Run

Use `run_climate_health_modeling.ipynb` to run the modeling scripts with notebook-specified inputs.
The expected input is the merged modeling dataset generated by `Generate_Modeling_Dataset.ipynb`, e.g.:

- `out/modeling/{NETWORK}_{MODE}_modeling_dataset.csv`

Scripts executed:
1. `climate_health_modeling_comprehensive.py` - Tests multiple feature sets and model families
2. `climate_health_modeling_parsimonious.py` - Theory-driven feature selection with hierarchical variance decomposition
3. `train_ml_models.py` - Full ML pipeline with 150+ features
4. `train_improved_models.py` - AR-augmented ML models with feature selection
5. `climate_health_modeling_anomalies.py` - Tests if climate anomalies predict disease anomalies

Outputs are written to `out/modeling/` with the notebook-specified prefix.

# Anomaly-Based Analysis

## Rationale

Climate and seasonal patterns are inherently correlated (e.g., hot temperatures occur in summer). To isolate the climate signal from shared seasonality, we tested whether **climate anomalies** (deviations from seasonal means) predict **disease anomalies** (deviations from seasonal means).

## Anomaly Computation

For each variable X at HSA h and week w:
```
X_anomaly(h,w) = X(h,w) - mean(X(h,week_of_year(w)))
```

This removes seasonal patterns from both climate and disease variables.

Disease anomalies are driven primarily by persistence (autoregressive dynamics), not weather anomalies.

# Discussion

## HSA Network Stability

The high temporal predictability (R² = 0.87 for INF, R² = 0.94 for NCD) reflects stable baseline disease burdens within each HSA:
- Mean lag-1 autocorrelation across HSAs: ~0.86 (INF), ~0.93 (NCD)
- Between-HSA variation accounts for majority of total variance
- Within-HSA persistence accounts for the remainder

## Climate Contribution

Autoregressive terms account for >91% of explained variance in both networks, capturing both between-HSA size differences and within-HSA persistence.

Climate variables provide minimal additional predictive value at weekly timescales:
- **INF**: 0.0% to +2.3% R² after seasonal controls (depending on model family)
- **NCD**: +0.1% to +2.5% R² after seasonal controls (depending on model family)

These findings are consistent with epidemiological understanding that short-term disease fluctuations are dominated by transmission dynamics, healthcare-seeking behavior, and reporting patterns rather than environmental drivers.

## Why Climate Contributes Little Beyond AR and Seasonality

1. **Shared Information**: Climate variables (temperature, precipitation) and seasonal controls (Fourier terms) both capture annual cycles. Once seasonality is controlled, climate adds little new information.

2. **Strong Persistence**: Week-to-week disease counts are highly correlated (r > 0.9 for NCD, r > 0.85 for INF). AR terms absorb most predictable variance.

3. **Indirect Pathways**: Climate effects on health operate through complex chains (temperature → behavior → exposure → infection → diagnosis → reporting) with substantial noise at each step.

4. **Weekly Aggregation**: Weekly temporal resolution may be too coarse to detect acute climate effects that manifest over days.

---

# Conclusions

1. The optimized HSA boundaries produce temporally stable surveillance units with high predictability (R² = 0.87 for INF, R² = 0.94 for NCD).

2. Climate variables contribute 0–2.5% to explained variance after controlling for autoregressive terms and seasonality, depending on model family.

3. Temperature variables (mean and max) provide the strongest climate signal for infectious disease; humidity and evaporation provide modest signals for hypertension.

4. Autoregressive terms dominate prediction (>91% of feature importance) in both networks.

5. Linear models (ElasticNet, Lasso, Ridge) outperform tree-based methods (Random Forest, Gradient Boosting).

6. Anomaly-based analysis confirms that climate contributions are minimal: disease anomalies are driven by persistence, not weather anomalies.
